<p>The recent <a href="http://www.technologyreview.com/web/25346/?a=f">Facebook vs. Your Privacy</a> uproar has ignited a firestorm of activity, of which <a href="http://www.joindiaspora.com/">Diaspora*</a> is the most recent viral player.&nbsp; (In related news: EFF responded today with <a href="http://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2010/05/bill-privacy-rights-social-network-users">A Bill of Privacy Rights for Social Network Users</a> but it continues under the assumption that your data is stored in each social network&#39;s data silo and is not generally available to you to manage and share exactly as you wish.)</p><!--break--><p>Conversations about people owning their online identity have been around for over 25 years, with <a href="http://www.broadcatch.com/">Broadcatch</a> (1994, with initial ideas presented in 1983) and <a href="http://www.openprivacy.org/">OpenPrivacy</a> (2001) providing two examples of early social/technological attempts to create such a system.&nbsp; (Disclaimer: I&#39;m sure there are others, but these are the ones that immediately come to mind as, well, I was the principal behind both.)&nbsp; Other, similar forays into this area include:</p><ul><li>&nbsp;<a href="http://www.get6d.com/">6D</a> is an identity-building application. Its purpose is to allow you to centralize your photos, thoughts, posts or anything else, but still share with friends, colleagues, or the world. (Their mission of &quot;You own your content&quot; reminds me of the WELL&#39;s famous &quot;You own your own words&quot; or &quot;YOYOW&quot; dating back to 1985.)</li><li><a href="http://onesocialweb.org/">Onesocialweb</a> is just like Facebook, but decentralized (the &#39;anti-Facebook&#39;?). Built on top of the realtime XMPP protocol, using open standards like Activitystreams, vcard4, etc.</li><li><a href="http://status.net/">OStatus</a> enables a decentralized micro-blogging environment (the &#39;anti-Twitter&#39;). They already have hundreds of federated servers and leverage open standards like XMPP, OAuth, etc.</li><li><a href="http://www.tonido.com/">Tonido</a> allows you to access and share your content directly through a web browser without uploading or worrying about storage limits.&nbsp; In short, run your own Personal Cloud.</li></ul><p><small><em>(A tip of the hat to Crosbie Fitch on the ProjectVRM list for these links)</em></small></p><p>Related efforts not specifically aimed at providing distributed secure identity include:</p><ul><li><a href="http://freenetproject.org/">The Freenet Project</a> has been providing a distributed, secure social network for over 20 years. From their home page: <em>&quot;Freenet is free software which lets you anonymously share files, browse and publish &quot;freesites&quot; (web sites accessible only through Freenet) and chat on forums, without fear of censorship. Freenet is decentralised to make it less vulnerable to attack, and if used in &quot;darknet&quot; mode, where users only connect to their friends, is very difficult to detect.&quot;</em></li><li><a href="http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/projectvrm/Main_Page">Project VRM</a> is leading much of the thinking that goes into this area.&nbsp; From the Project VRM site:<em> &quot;In a narrow sense, VRM (or Vendor Relationship Management) is the reciprocal &mdash; the customer side &mdash; of CRM (or Customer Relationship Management).&quot;</em></li></ul><p>The key issue in creating the &quot;holy grail&quot; of a decentralized online social networking service is the ability for people to own their own data.&nbsp; (Free and open source software, transparent operations, choice of services, anonymity, pseudonimity and strongly verifiable identity are also key issues, but they are implementation details, some of which I&#39;ve already discussed in <a href="http://fen.net/blog/2005/05/02/four-more-laws-identity">Four More Laws of Identity</a>.)&nbsp; To facilitate this, a decentralized and secure identity solution must exist that enables people to authenticate as service providers and selectively share their data.<br />
<br />
<a href="http://openid.net/">OpenID</a> and <a href="http://oauth.net/">OAuth</a> are the major players in this field, but as Joe Andrieu recently wrote on the ProjectVRM mailing list: &#39;I think it&#39;s vastly more important that the &quot;default&quot; for online activity support a baseline standard of liberty and free association. And, frankly, I don&#39;t think we have that yet.&#39;&nbsp; Indeed, the &quot;baseline standard&quot; should enable 100% security from the outset, though specific implementations - such as a company HR database - are free to weaken the protocol in ways that they hopefully clearly describe before any data is entered.</p><p>A basic problem with these protocols is that they are strongly tied to DNS which can be subverted in localized applications, such as within a corporate or educational institution.&nbsp; While DNS certainly provides ease of use and a low barrier to entry, I believe that an identity and personal profile management system that we expect people to feel safe using should allow for 100% security at the core.&nbsp; Systems like Freenet are a step in the security direction, but secure identity remains elusive.</p><p>The good news is that there is a set of identity and data sharing mechanisms that meet all the requirements, namely the <a href="http://www.oasis-open.org/committees/documents.php?wg_abbrev=xri">Extensible Resource Identifier</a> (XRI) and <a href="http://www.oasis-open.org/committees/documents.php?wg_abbrev=xdi">XRI Data Interchange</a> (XDI) protocols.&nbsp; While these typically (to date) run on top of DNS, they are transport- and resource-discovery agnostic, enabling then to run on top of Freenet, <a href="http://gnunet.org/">GNUnet</a> or even a private flat-file spreadsheet!&nbsp; Initial social networks based on XRI could be simple, employing DNS and supporting one name per user, but there&#39;s nothing stopping it from evolving into an OpenPrivacy-like pseudonymous network with <a href="http://civicactions.com/blog/dont_forget_reputation">authenticated reputations</a>.</p><p>Many who have been burned by Facebook and its ilk are looking toward <a href="http://dig.csail.mit.edu/2008/Papers/MSNWS/">decentralization as an escape from those walled gardens</a>.&nbsp; So now is the time to look again at a technology that&#39;s been passed over once (XRI existed long before OpenID, but it was still a specification in progress in the OASIS Standards body) and rally behind it to create true user-centric digital identity.&nbsp; It is key to what Diaspora* is trying to do.<br />
&nbsp;</p>
